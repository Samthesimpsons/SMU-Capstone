\section{Results and Analysis}
\label{sec:results}

We present the four research questions as a diagnostic-to-remedy chain. RQ1 establishes that profile-discordance is prevalent and structural, RQ2 shows that it carries a measurable return penalty, RQ3 audits where the existing baselines sit on the coherence axis, and RQ4 demonstrates a training-time remedy that closes the gap.

\subsection{RQ1: Profile-Discordance is Prevalent and Structural}
\label{sec:results:rq1}

We characterise discordance in four slices, starting from the pooled transaction rate and drilling into the customer, band, and year dimensions in turn. At the transaction level (Figure~\ref{fig:rq1-dist}), of 228{,}241 scoreable Buys:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item 18.6\% violate the declared band by two or more steps.
    \item 81.4\% are coherent, of which 34.1\% strictly ($d = 0$).
    \item Mean discordance is 0.874 bands across all scoreable Buys, indicating that when mismatches occur they are usually a single band step.
\end{enumerate}

\begin{figure}[H]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=\linewidth]{figures/rq1_discordance_distribution.pdf}
    \caption{Distribution of pairwise discordance $d$ over 228{,}241 scoreable Buys. Most mass sits at $d \leq 1$ (coherent), with non-negligible density at $d \geq 2$ (discordant).}
    \label{fig:rq1-dist}
\end{figure}

The pooled rate cannot tell us whether these violations are i.i.d.\ transaction-level slips spread thinly across the customer base or a structural trait concentrated in a subset. Slicing by customer settles the question. The per-customer discordant share (Figure~\ref{fig:rq1-self}) is sharply bimodal, concentrating in the lowest and highest bins rather than clustering near the 18.6\% population mean. More explicitly, 64.4\% of banded customers are fully coherent across every Buy and 17.2\% are fully discordant. Hartigan's dip test on the 28{,}770 per-customer values rejects unimodality ($D = 0.086$, $p < 10^{-15}$), confirming that discordance is a persistent customer-level trait rather than i.i.d.\ transaction-level noise.

\begin{figure}[H]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=\linewidth]{figures/rq1_self_discordance.pdf}
    \caption{Per-customer share of discordant Buys is sharply bimodal, concentrating in the lowest and highest bins and ruling out i.i.d.\ transaction-level noise.}
    \label{fig:rq1-self}
\end{figure}

Since the trait is customer-level, the next question is which customers carry it. Coherence per declared band is U-shaped (Figure~\ref{fig:rq1-per-band}). The two centre bands sit at $\sim$90\%, while Conservative drops to 45.1\% (possibly reflecting a ``reach for yield'' tendency) and Aggressive to 56.2\% (possibly reflecting a regression toward safer assets). This concentration of mismatch at the ordinal extremes corroborates the bimodality finding from the customer slice.

\begin{figure}[H]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=\linewidth]{figures/rq1_per_band_coherence.pdf}
    \caption{Coherence share by declared band. The U-shape supports the bimodality result of Figure~\ref{fig:rq1-self}: discordance concentrates on the ordinal extremes.}
    \label{fig:rq1-per-band}
\end{figure}

A final slice rules out a regime-driven artefact. Mean discordance is flat across 2019 to 2022 (within 0.02 bands, see Figure~\ref{fig:rq1-yearly}), so the pattern is not the product of any single year's market conditions. The 2018 value (0.98) sits higher but covers a small slice. MiFID II came into force on 2018-01-03, the exact start of FAR-Trans, so we hypothesise the elevation as a transition effect. However, this hypothesis is not tested and is potential future work.

    \label{fig:rq1-yearly}
\end{figure}

\subsection{RQ2: Coherent Buys Earn a Conditional Return Premium}
\label{sec:results:rq2}

Having established that discordance is a persistent customer-level trait concentrated at the ordinal extremes, the natural follow-up is whether it matters economically: do discordant Buys actually earn less?

RQ2 asks whether profile-coherent Buys earn different realised returns from discordant ones, after controlling for asset volatility, customer segment, and calendar year. We test this via the OLS specification in Equation~\ref{eq:rq2-ols}, where the coefficient of interest is $\beta_1$ on \texttt{is\_coherent}. The null hypothesis $H_0\colon \beta_1 = 0$ states that coherence has no conditional effect on 6-month returns, and we evaluate it with a two-sided Wald test at $\alpha = 0.05$ using cluster-robust standard errors on \texttt{customerID}. The model is fit on 208{,}029 priced Buys, and Table~\ref{tab:rq2-full} reports the full coefficient table.

Coherent Buys earn $+2.94$ percentage points (pp) higher 6-month realised return than discordant ones, conditional on volatility, segment, and year. The positive sign with $p < 10^{-12}$ provides strong evidence against the prior that regulatory coherence imposes a return tax. The raw slice gap is $+3.31$ pp, so controls absorb only $\sim$0.4 pp ($\sim$11\% of the unconditional gap). This implies that coherence is not a volatility, segment, or year confound. However, it is important to note that unobserved confounders such as financial literacy, advisor channel, and account constraints are not ruled out. The model's adjusted $R^2$ is 0.051, meaning 95\% of return variance is driven by factors outside the specification; this is typical of cross-sectional return regressions where idiosyncratic price movements dominate. The low $R^2$ does not affect coefficient inference: valid hypothesis testing requires correctly specified standard errors, not high explanatory power, and the cluster-robust SEs account for within-customer correlation across the 27{,}873 customer clusters.

The volatility coefficient is $-10.3$ pp per unit, consistent with low-volatility outperformance over 2019 to 2022 and the 2022 rate shock that hit high-beta names. All four year dummies reject $H_0$, reflecting distinct market regimes relative to the 2018 reference year. The 2019 coefficient captures the equity recovery from the late-2018 selloff, 2020 is the strongest at $+21.1$ pp driven by the post-COVID V-shaped rebound, and 2021 reflects continued recovery under accommodative monetary policy before late-2021 rate-hike expectations set in. The 2022 coefficient turns negative at $-2.0$ pp as aggressive Fed hikes triggered a broad fixed-income drawdown. All four \texttt{customerType} dummies fail to reject $H_0$, indicating that segmentation contributes no detectable return signal. Together with the bimodality found in RQ1, this suggests that customer type does not capture the persistent discordance trait either. The coherence premium therefore survives all controls we can measure, motivating the question of whether existing recommenders produce coherent lists at all.

\subsection{RQ3: Both Baselines Under-Serve at Least One Band}
\label{sec:results:rq3}

With a confirmed return premium for coherent Buys, we now audit whether the two FAR-Trans baselines actually deliver profile-coherent recommendations across all four risk bands.

RQ3 asks whether the two FAR-Trans baselines produce profile-coherent recommendations across all four risk bands. We begin by comparing their aggregate performance at primary-metric optima, summarised in Table~\ref{tab:rq3-aggregate}. LightGCN wins on ranking quality with nDCG@10 of 0.330, Recall@10 of 0.495, and PC@10 of 0.794, but loses on ROI@10 at $-0.5$\%/mo. Conversely, Random Forest wins on ROI@10 at $+1.4$\%/mo but ranks near random on nDCG@10 of 0.019 and Recall@10 of 0.036. Both appear adequate on aggregate PC@10, with LightGCN at $1.17\times$ lift and Random Forest at $1.06\times$, but the aggregate hides per-band failures.

Measured against the full FAR-Trans customer base (28{,}770 banded customers), LightGCN under-serves the 19.7\% Conservative segment while Random Forest under-serves 60.9\% (Conservative and Income combined). Aggressive's high lift is partly mechanical: $\pi(\text{Aggressive}) = 0.35$ is the lowest band, so any model skewing higher-volatility clears that bar easily. Neither baseline has band labels or a band-aware loss, so per-band coherence is a passive byproduct of training rather than a controlled output.

\subsection{RQ4: Stratification + PC-Loss Closes the Per-Band Deficit}
\label{sec:results:rq4}

RQ3 showed that both baselines under-serve at least one risk band, with the deficit concentrated on the Conservative segment for LightGCN and on both Conservative and Income for Random Forest. RQ4 asks whether a band-aware architecture can close this gap without sacrificing ranking quality or return. PC-LightGCN (Section~\ref{sec:method:pclgcn}) tackles this by routing each customer to a band-stratified sub-model and adding a coherence margin to the BPR objective. We evaluate two ablations against the baseline LightGCN: $\lambda = 0$, which isolates the effect of stratification alone, and $\lambda = 1$, which activates the coherence margin on top of stratification.

Table~\ref{tab:rq4-aggregate} reports aggregate metrics for the three trials averaged over 69 splits. Figure~\ref{fig:rq4-winrate} complements the table by showing the per-split win rate of each ablation against the baseline, defined as the fraction of the 69 splits on which the trial outperforms the comparator (Section~\ref{sec:setup:stats}). This indicates how consistently each trial wins rather than whether a single strong split drives the aggregate. Figure~\ref{fig:rq4-deltas} goes one step further, plotting the paired per-split deltas so the magnitude and direction of each metric shift are visible split by split.

The headline result is that the $\lambda = 1$ treatment lifts PC@10 from 0.794 to 0.918, a $+12.42$ pp gain, and PC-lift from $1.17\times$ to $1.40\times$. This improvement is consistent across all temporal splits: Figure~\ref{fig:rq4-winrate} shows a 100\% win rate on PC@10 across all 69 splits, ruling out the possibility that a few strong splits drive the aggregate.

Because the treatment bundles two architectural changes, the $\lambda = 0$ ablation lets us isolate how much each piece contributes to the $+12.42$ pp PC@10 gain. Stratification alone supplies $+2.75$ pp of PC@10, about a quarter of the full gain. Importantly, it does so at virtually no cost to the other metrics. The nDCG@10 delta is $+0.0022$ with a 56.5\% win rate, Recall@10 shifts by $+0.0063$ with a 72.5\% win rate, and ROI@10 moves by $-0.0019$ pp/mo with a 27.5\% win rate. All three deltas are small enough that none registers as a meaningful effect, making stratification the cheap component of the intervention.

Adding the coherence margin on top buys the remaining $+9.68$ pp of PC@10, roughly four-fifths of the total, winning on every split. This larger gain comes at a measurable but bounded ranking cost of $-2.23$ pp of nDCG@10 and $-3.38$ pp of Recall@10, both lost on every split.

Aggregate ROI@10 is essentially flat across all three trials. The stratification ablation shifts ROI@10 by $-0.0019$ pp/mo with a 27.5\% win rate, while the full treatment shifts it by $-0.0018$ pp/mo with a 33.3\% win rate. The coherence margin step on its own contributes a near-zero $+0.0002$ pp/mo with a 58.0\% win rate. The small ROI cost therefore lives in the stratification step rather than the coherence margin, and is too small to distinguish from zero in Table~\ref{tab:rq4-aggregate}.

Together with the coherence premium established in RQ2, this null aggregate-ROI effect indicates that the recommender can be made meaningfully more profile-coherent without giving up the return story.

Table~\ref{tab:rq4-per-band} decomposes PC-lift@10 by declared band to trace how the intervention resolves the per-band deficit from RQ3. Conservative, which sat at $0.75\times$ under the baseline, climbs to $0.91\times$ under stratification alone but remains below the random baseline; only after adding the coherence margin does it cross to $1.19\times$. Aggressive exhibits a non-monotonic pattern: stratification alone reduces lift from $1.35\times$ to $1.17\times$, likely because the smaller Aggressive training graph yields lower-quality embeddings in isolation, but the coherence margin more than recovers the loss at $1.97\times$. Income and Balanced, already above-random under the baseline, improve modestly without being sacrificed. Under $\lambda = 1$, all four bands clear the random baseline for the first time.
